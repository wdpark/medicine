- Subpulmonic Effusion
	- Free flowing below diaphragm
	- Frontal view: Pseudo highest point of hemidiaphragm is displaced laterally
	- Lateral view: Curved hemidiaphragm becomes flat anteriorly

- Costophrenic Angle Blunting
	- Normal pleural fluid about 2-10 mL
	- Frontal view: lateral costophrenic angle about 300 mL
	- Lateral view: posterior costophrenic angle about 75 mL

- Meniscus Sign
	- Pleural fluid level shaped like meniscus ("U")

- Loculated Effusions
	- Limited mobility of pleural effusion often due to adhesions, old empyema, or hemothorax
	- Do not change appearance with positional changes
	- Difficult to drain due to non-communicating pockets

- Fissural Pseudotumors
	- Sharply marginated, lenticular shape collections of pleural fluid
	- Interlobar pulmonary fissure most common
	- Not free flowing

- Laminar Effusion
	- Thin, band-like density along lateral chest wall
	- Near costophrenic angle
	- Not free flowing

- Hydropneumothorax
	- Both pneumothorax (look air) and fluid in pleural space
	- Air-fluid level
	- Etiology: trauma, surgery, bronchopleural fistula
