**Hyponatremia**
- Serum and urine osmolality + assess volume status if hypotonic
- Increase sodium by 4-6 mEq/L in 24 hrs
- Can consider simultaneous desmopressin because it prevents unexpected water diuresis leading to rapid sodium increase
- Fluid restriction? -> Demeclocycline (can use chronically, but side effects of photosensitivity and GI symptoms) or tolvaptan (expensive, can only use <1 month due to liver damage)
	- Adding loop diuretics wwo salt supplementation is no diff
- Symptomatic isovolemic hypotonic hyponatremia -> 100 ml bolus of 3% saline

**Hypernatremia**
- Most common cause is loss of hypotonic body fluids with inadequate water replacement due to lack of access or thirst
- Decrease sodium by <0.5 mEq/hr with goal of 10-12 mEq/L in 24 hrs to prevent cerebral edema
- 2/2 intravascular volume depletion? -> NS
	- Otherwise 1/2 NS
- Calculate free water deficit
- Central DI -> desmopressin
- Nephrogenic DI -> thiazides (basically shifts water reabsorption more proximally instead of collecting duct)
	- Lithium-induced -> amiloride (reduces lithium uptake)

**Hypokalemia**
- K > 3 usually asymptomatic
- Symptoms: weakness, paralysis, GI hypomotility, cardiac arrhythmias
- ECG: ST segment depression, decreased T wave amplitude, increased U wave amplitude
- Severe hypokalemia can lead to rhabdomyolysis
- Insulin and beta agonists push K into cells
- 24 hr urine K is gold standard, but often impractical
	- Due potassium-creatinine ratio instead where <13 mEq/g suggests hypokalemia 2/2 lack of intake, transcellular shifts, or GI losses
